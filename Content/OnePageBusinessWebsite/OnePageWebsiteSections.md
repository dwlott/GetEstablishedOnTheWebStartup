# One-Page Website Sections

Use this structure for the default simplest business website output.

This is a content outline, not a website build. Keep it plain Markdown and framework-neutral.

The default order should stay simple. A future draft can change the order for a specific business type, but the first pass should avoid creating extra pages or complex navigation.

## Recommended Section Order

### 1. Hero

Purpose: Quickly explain who the business helps and what action the visitor should take.

Source fields:

- Site Title.
- Business Name.
- Tagline.
- Short Business Description.
- Call To Action.

Draft guidance:

- Keep the message direct.
- Use the clearest confirmed call to action.
- Do not include final pricing or claims unless approved.

### 2. Services

Purpose: Show what the business does in plain language.

Source fields:

- Main Services.
- Supporting Services.
- Service Areas.

Draft guidance:

- Start with the main services only.
- Keep descriptions short.
- Put unconfirmed services in open decisions instead of publishing them as final.

### 3. About

Purpose: Give the visitor enough context to understand who is behind the work.

Source fields:

- About Us.
- Origin Or Background Notes.
- Values Or Working Style.

Draft guidance:

- Explain who is behind the business.
- Keep the origin story simple.
- Do not invent personal history, credentials, or experience.

### 4. Why Choose Us

Purpose: Give practical reasons to trust the business without unsupported hype.

Source fields:

- Why Choose Us.
- Credentials Or Experience Notes.
- Examples, Screenshots, Or Portfolio Notes.

Draft guidance:

- Use practical reasons, not hype.
- Prefer specific proof over broad claims.
- Leave unconfirmed credentials out of public copy.

### 5. Testimonials Or Proof

Purpose: Support the offer with real evidence when available.

Source fields:

- Testimonials Or Proof.
- Examples.
- Repository or project artifacts when relevant.

If proof is not ready, keep this section as a draft note or move the question to `Plans/OpenQuestions.md`.

Do not invent testimonials, results, client names, project examples, or screenshots.

### 6. Frequently Asked Questions

Purpose: Answer common concerns before the visitor reaches out.

Source fields:

- Frequently asked questions.
- Service details.
- Process notes.
- Location or service area notes.

Draft guidance:

- Use questions real visitors are likely to ask.
- Keep answers short.
- Avoid legal, financial, or policy promises unless the business owner provides approved language.

### 7. Contact

Purpose: Make the next step clear.

Source fields:

- Contact Information.
- Preferred Contact Method.
- Hours.
- Location Or Service Area.
- Social Links.
- Call To Action.

Draft guidance:

- Make the next step obvious.
- Use only confirmed contact details.
- Keep forms, booking tools, and platform-specific contact widgets out of scope until publishing is chosen.

### 8. Footer

Purpose: Close the page with basic business information and placeholders for future policy needs.

Source fields:

- Footer Text.
- Copyright Or Business Name Line.
- Legal Or Policy Placeholders Needed Later.

Draft guidance:

- Use a simple business name or copyright line if confirmed.
- Treat legal, privacy, accessibility, cookie, and policy content as placeholders until reviewed by the business owner or an appropriate professional.

## Draft Review Checklist

Before using these sections to create a one-page draft, check:

- The essential inputs are filled in.
- Unknowns are blank or listed as open decisions.
- The page can work as one page without child pages.
- The content avoids unsupported hype.
- Testimonials, proof, credentials, pricing, policies, and contact details are confirmed before public use.
- The content stays plain Markdown and does not choose a publishing platform.

## Future Child Pages

Child pages should be considered only after the one-page draft is useful and a section needs more room.

Possible future child pages:

- Services.
- About.
- Contact.
- Frequently Asked Questions.
- Testimonials or case studies.
- Resources or blog.

Do not create child pages during the first one-page planning pass.
