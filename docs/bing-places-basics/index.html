Bing Places Basics

Get established on Bing — a free next step for showing up in Bing Search and Bing Maps.

Bing Places for Business is the free listing platform Microsoft provides for business owners to create, claim, and manage how a business appears on Bing. This page walks you through claiming or creating one: what to do, what to gather first, and the official Bing links — without promising rankings or results. Treat it as one practical external profile, not a full local search plan.

Every official Bing link you need to get established on Bing is on this page — follow the checklist below, one step at a time.

Basic Starting Path

You do not need a formal business plan, but at least a short sketch of what you do, who you serve, and how customers reach you will make the steps below much easier.

  1. Gather at least the minimal information you will need — business name, category, address or service area, phone number, hours, and website. Use Basic Information To Gather below as your checklist.
  2. Open Bing Places for Business.
  3. Sign in with a Microsoft account the business owner controls.
  4. Search for the business before creating anything new.
  5. If the business already appears, claim or manage the existing listing rather than creating a duplicate.
  6. If the business does not appear, create a new business listing and enter the core profile details.
  7. If you already manage a Google Business Profile, look for Bing's import option in the Bing Places flow so you can use existing listing data where available.
  8. Complete the verification method Bing offers for that listing.
  9. Confirm the published details: business name, category, address or service area, phone number, website, hours, description, and photos.
  10. Keep the listing current when hours, services, location, or ownership change.

Basic Information To Gather

  • Business name as it is used in the real world.
  • Business category.
  • Storefront address, service area, or hybrid service-area details.
  • Phone number.
  • Website URL.
  • Hours.
  • Business description.
  • Services or products.
  • Logo, cover photo, and useful business photos.
  • Microsoft account owner and backup manager decisions.
  • Google Business Profile access if you plan to import existing listing details.
  • Verification contact: the person who can receive phone, email, text, mail, or dashboard verification prompts if Bing asks for them.

How Bing Places Listing Works

Bing Places for Business lets a business owner manage core listing details for Bing Search and Bing Maps. Bing may already have a listing from public or third-party data sources, so the first job is to search and claim the right business rather than creating a duplicate.

For many small businesses, Bing is a practical follow-up after Google because some customers use Bing directly, and Microsoft services may surface Bing business information. Bing's owner portal may also let you import details from Google Business Profile, which can reduce duplicate typing.

Verification options can vary by listing. Use the methods shown in your Bing Places dashboard and keep the account owner information documented so the business can maintain the listing later.

Copy For A Website Section

Bing Places for Business can be part of a basic online-presence setup for a local business. Start by searching for the business in Bing Places, claim an existing listing if one appears, or create a new listing if needed. If you already manage a Google Business Profile, check whether Bing can import the existing information.

After verification, keep the business name, category, address or service area, phone number, website, hours, description, and photos accurate. Treat it as a simple visibility step: free, useful, and worth keeping current.
